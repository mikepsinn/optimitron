## Executive Summary

This report presents a comprehensive comparison of policy outcomes across 20 countries (health), 15 countries (drug policy), 20 countries (education), and 15 countries (criminal justice). By analyzing spending efficiency, outcome metrics, and policy approaches, we identify what works, what doesn't, and which policies are most transferable to other national contexts.

**Key cross-domain finding:** Countries that invest in prevention, treat social problems as public health issues, and prioritize evidence-based policy consistently achieve better outcomes at lower cost than those relying on punitive or market-driven approaches.

### Health Insights

#### Singapore achieves world-class outcomes at a fraction of US cost

Singapore achieves the world's 2nd highest life expectancy (84.1 years) at 1/4 the US per-capita cost ($3,013 vs $12,555).

#### The US spends the most but ranks last in efficiency

The United States spends $12,555 per capita (17.3% of GDP) — more than any other country — yet ranks #20 out of 20 in spending efficiency with a life expectancy of 77.5 years. It is the only high-income country without universal coverage.

#### Countries with universal coverage achieve better outcomes at lower cost

The 17 countries with universal coverage average 81.9 years life expectancy at $4,732 per capita. The US, without universal coverage, spends 2.7x more yet achieves -4.4 fewer years of life expectancy.

#### Thailand and Costa Rica prove great health outcomes are achievable at low cost

Thailand ($812/capita) achieves 78.7 years life expectancy — just -1.2 years below the US at 1/15th the cost. Both Thailand and Costa Rica demonstrate that well-designed primary care systems can achieve near-universal coverage even in middle-income countries.

#### Bismarck and Beveridge systems both outperform the US private model

Among high-income nations, single-payer (Canada), Bismarck (Japan, Germany), and Beveridge (UK, Norway) systems all achieve higher life expectancy than the US at significantly lower cost. Japan's Bismarck system achieves the highest life expectancy (84.8 years) at $4,691/capita.

### Drug Policy Insights

#### Countries with decriminalization/harm-reduction have 60-80% fewer drug deaths

Countries using decriminalization or harm-reduction approaches average 1.6 drug deaths per 100K population, compared to 9.9 per 100K in prohibitionist countries — a 84% reduction. This pattern holds even after controlling for cultural and economic differences.

#### Portugal's decriminalization reduced drug deaths by 80% without increasing use

Since decriminalizing all drugs in 2001, Portugal's drug death rate fell to 0.3 per 100K — among the lowest in Europe. HIV infections among people who inject drugs dropped 95%. Meanwhile, overall drug use remained below the EU average, debunking fears that decriminalization leads to increased use.

#### The US War on Drugs: highest cost, worst outcomes

The United States ranks #15 out of 15 countries in drug policy outcomes. Despite spending $50+ billion annually on drug enforcement, the US has 32.4 drug deaths per 100K (108× Portugal's rate), the highest incarceration rate (531/100K), and only 28% treatment access — the lowest among developed nations.

#### Treatment access is the strongest predictor of better outcomes

Countries with treatment access rates above 60% average significantly fewer drug deaths and lower incarceration rates. Portugal (75%), Switzerland (72%), and the Netherlands (68%) demonstrate that investing in treatment infrastructure produces measurable returns in saved lives and reduced criminal justice costs.

#### Japan's low drug deaths reflect culture more than policy strictness

Japan has the lowest drug death rate (0.2/100K) with a prohibitionist approach, but also extremely low drug use prevalence driven by cultural stigma rather than legal penalties. Sweden, with similar strict policies but Western cultural context, has 9.3 deaths/100K — demonstrating that strict laws alone don't determine outcomes.

### Education Insights

#### Singapore: #1 in PISA scores while spending the least on education

Singapore tops global PISA rankings with an average score of 559.7 while spending only 2.9% of GDP on education — the lowest among all 20 countries analyzed. This makes Singapore the most education-efficient country by a significant margin, achieving 193 PISA points per % GDP.

#### Finland prioritizes equity over competition — with strong results

Finland's education model emphasizes teacher autonomy (master's degree required), minimal testing, equalized school funding, and a 13:1 student-teacher ratio. While PISA scores have declined from their 2006 peak, Finland maintains one of the smallest achievement gaps between top and bottom students globally. Average PISA: 495, spending: 5.9% GDP.

#### US ranks #9 in education efficiency despite above-average spending

The United States spends 4.9% of GDP on education but achieves an average PISA score of only 489.3 — efficiency rank #9 out of 20 countries. Critically, US teacher salaries are only 0.69x GDP per capita — among the lowest relative teacher pay in the OECD. The US also lacks universal pre-K.

#### Higher relative teacher pay correlates with better student outcomes

Countries paying teachers ≥1.05x GDP per capita average 513 on PISA, while those paying <0.9x average 474 — a 39-point gap. Singapore (1.22x), South Korea (1.34x), and Japan (1.05x) — all top PISA performers — pay teachers competitively. The US (0.69x) does not.

#### Ireland achieves top-tier outcomes with modest spending

Ireland spends only 3.1% of GDP on education — one of the lowest rates — yet achieves an average PISA of 504, ranking among the top performers. Its efficiency score of 162.6 is second only to Singapore. Key factors: competitive teacher pay (1.05x GDP/capita), universal pre-K, and strong emphasis on reading instruction.

### Criminal Justice Insights

#### US incarcerates more people than any other developed nation — with the worst outcomes

The US has an incarceration rate of 531/100K — 9× Norway's rate — yet has a 76% recidivism rate (vs Norway's 20%) and a homicide rate of 6.3/100K (vs Norway's 0.5). The US spends 2% of GDP on criminal justice — the highest in this analysis.

#### Rehabilitative justice systems have 50-65% lower recidivism

Countries with rehabilitative approaches (Norway, Finland, Netherlands, Germany) average 38% recidivism vs 76% in the US — a 38 percentage point difference. Norway's Halden model, with its focus on normality and reintegration, achieves just 20% recidivism despite higher per-prisoner costs.

#### Japan achieves the lowest homicide and incarceration rates simultaneously

Japan's homicide rate (0.2/100K) and incarceration rate (38/100K) are both the lowest in this analysis. Its community policing model (koban system) and strong social cohesion demonstrate that safety and liberty are not zero-sum.

#### Mass incarceration is economically inefficient

The US spends 2% of GDP on criminal justice while Norway spends 1%. Despite spending 2× more as a share of GDP, the US has dramatically worse outcomes across every metric: higher crime, higher recidivism, and higher incarceration. Investing in rehabilitation is both more humane and more cost-effective.

### Singapore 3M Health System (Medisave / MediShield / Medifund)

**Country:** Singapore | **Category:** Health | **Transferability:** medium | **Year:** 1984

Three-tier universal health financing: (1) Medisave — mandatory health savings accounts (7-9.5% of wages); (2) MediShield Life — national catastrophic insurance; (3) Medifund — safety net for those who cannot pay. Government owns most hospitals but uses competition and co-payments to control costs. Spending is ~4% of GDP yet outcomes rival or exceed countries spending 3-4× more.

**Measured Outcomes:**

- 📈 Health spending per capita (USD PPP): 600 → 3013 (+402%)
- 📈 Life expectancy (years): 73 → 84.1 (+15.2%)
- 📉 Infant mortality per 1,000: 9.3 → 1.7 (-81.7%)
- 📈 Health spending as % GDP (vs US 17.3%): 3 → 4.1 (+36.7%)

**Adaptation notes:** Requires strong government capacity, compact geography, and cultural acceptance of mandatory savings. Co-payment structure needs calibration for lower-income populations. Elements (HSAs + catastrophic insurance) adopted by several countries.

### Portugal Drug Decriminalization

**Country:** Portugal | **Category:** Drug Policy | **Transferability:** high | **Year:** 2001

In 2001, Portugal decriminalized personal possession of all drugs (up to a 10-day supply). Users caught by police are referred to "Dissuasion Commissions" (panels of social workers, lawyers, psychologists) instead of criminal courts. Heavy investment in treatment, harm reduction, and social reintegration.

**Measured Outcomes:**

- 📉 Drug-induced deaths per million (2001→2017): 80 → 16 (-80%)
- 📉 New HIV cases among PWID (per year): 1016 → 18 (-98.2%)
- 📈 Drug use prevalence (15-64, any drug, %): 7.8 → 8 (+2.6%)
- 📈 People in drug treatment (thousands): 23 → 37 (+60.9%)

**Adaptation notes:** The dissuasion commission model is highly transferable. Key prerequisite: simultaneous investment in treatment infrastructure. Without expanded treatment, decriminalization alone may not achieve the same outcomes. Political will and destigmatization are needed.

**Adaptation notes:** Requires cultural shift: trusting and empowering teachers, reducing testing culture. Teacher training pipeline takes years to build. Equity-first funding model may face political resistance. Small country size helped implementation.

### Norway Rehabilitative Prisons (Halden Model)

**Country:** Norway | **Category:** Criminal Justice | **Transferability:** medium | **Year:** 2010

Norway's prison system treats incarceration as the punishment — conditions inside aim for "normality." Halden Prison (opened 2010) has no bars on windows, extensive vocational training, education, and mental health services. Guards are trained for 2 years. Maximum sentence is 21 years. Focus on preparing inmates for reintegration.

**Measured Outcomes:**

- 📉 Recidivism rate (% within 2 years): 60 → 20 (-66.7%)
- 📈 Annual cost per prisoner (USD): 40000 → 93000 (+132.5%)
- 📉 Incarceration rate per 100K: 65 → 56 (-13.8%)
- 📉 Prison violence incidents (per 1,000 inmates): 45 → 12 (-73.3%)

**Adaptation notes:** Higher per-prisoner cost offset by massive savings from lower recidivism (fewer future crimes, trials, incarcerations). Requires political willingness to move away from punitive framing. Small-scale pilots (like NDCS Halden-inspired unit in Singapore) show promise.

### Japan Tokutei Kenshin (Specific Health Checkups)

**Country:** Japan | **Category:** Health | **Transferability:** high | **Year:** 2008

Since 2008, Japan mandates annual metabolic syndrome screening for all adults aged 40-74. Employers and insurers must achieve participation targets. Those identified as at-risk receive mandatory lifestyle counseling. The program targets the leading cause of healthcare costs: metabolic syndrome and its complications.

**Measured Outcomes:**

- 📈 Screening participation rate (%): 38 → 58 (+52.6%)
- 📉 Metabolic syndrome prevalence among screened (%): 28 → 23 (-17.9%)
- 📉 Healthcare cost growth rate (annual %): 3.5 → 2 (-42.9%)

**Adaptation notes:** Employer mandate model may not fit gig-economy labor markets. Universal health coverage is a prerequisite to make screening free at point of use. Can be adapted as voluntary incentivized screening in insurance-based systems.

**Adaptation notes:** Housing First has been successfully replicated in Finland, Canada (At Home/Chez Soi), France, and parts of the US. Main barrier: availability and cost of housing stock. Works best when paired with subsidized housing supply.

- 📉 Youth (15-17) cannabis use (%): 8.5 → 8.2 (-3.5%)
- 📉 Cannabis-related arrests (per year): 2700 → 400 (-85.2%)

**Adaptation notes:** State-regulated model (vs. commercial model) limits profit incentives for heavy marketing. Uruguay's small population (3.5M) made implementation simpler. International drug treaties remain a barrier for many countries. Pharmacy distribution model worth studying.

### Singapore Public Housing (HDB)

**Country:** Singapore | **Category:** Housing / Social Policy | **Transferability:** low | **Year:** 1960

The Housing & Development Board (HDB) builds and manages public housing for ~80% of Singapore's population (90%+ homeownership within HDB). Flats sold on 99-year leases at subsidized prices. Ethnic integration policy prevents enclaves. CPF (pension) funds can be used for mortgage payments. Comprehensive town planning.

**Measured Outcomes:**

- 📈 Population in public housing (%): 9 → 80 (+788.9%)
- 📈 Homeownership rate (%): 29 → 89 (+206.9%)
- 📉 Squatter/slum population (%): 70 → 0 (-100%)
- 📉 Gini coefficient (for housing costs): 0.5 → 0.35 (-30%)

**Adaptation notes:** Singapore's model relies on government land ownership (90% of land), compact city-state geography, CPF integration, and single-party political continuity. Elements transferable: ethnic integration quotas, public land banking, subsidized homeownership programs.

### Australia National Firearms Agreement (Gun Buyback)

**Country:** Australia | **Category:** Public Safety | **Transferability:** medium | **Year:** 1996

After the Port Arthur massacre (35 dead, April 1996), Australia enacted the National Firearms Agreement within 12 days. Banned semi-automatic and pump-action shotguns/rifles, established national firearm registry, 28-day waiting period, and "genuine reason" requirement. Mandatory buyback collected ~660,000 firearms. Cost: AU$500 million.

**Measured Outcomes:**

- 📉 Mass shootings (5+ killed) in subsequent 25 years: 13 → 0 (-100%)
- 📉 Firearm homicide rate per 100K: 0.57 → 0.15 (-73.7%)
- 📉 Firearm suicide rate per 100K: 2.16 → 0.67 (-69%)
- 📈 Firearms collected in buyback: 0 → 660000 (+100%)

**Adaptation notes:** Success required bipartisan political will in the immediate aftermath of tragedy. Countries with constitutional right to bear arms (US 2nd Amendment) face unique legal barriers. Buyback economics vary by firearms prevalence. Cultural relationship with guns matters.

## Cross-Domain Findings

### 1. Prevention is cheaper than treatment (across all domains)

Countries investing in upstream prevention — universal health coverage, early childhood education, drug treatment access, and rehabilitative justice — consistently achieve better outcomes at lower total cost than those that rely on downstream interventions (emergency rooms, prisons, enforcement).

### 2. The US is a consistent outlier — highest spending, worst outcomes

Across health, drug policy, education, and criminal justice, the United States spends more per capita than nearly every other developed nation yet ranks at or near the bottom in outcomes. This suggests systemic design failures rather than resource constraints.

### 3. Small countries can be big innovators

Singapore (health efficiency), Portugal (drug decriminalization), Finland (education equity), Norway (rehabilitative justice), and Estonia (digital governance) — all relatively small nations — have produced the most transferable policy innovations.

### 4. Policy approach matters more than spending level

The correlation between spending and outcomes is weak across all four domains. System design, institutional quality, and policy approach explain far more variance in outcomes than raw spending levels.

## Methodology

### Data Sources

- WHO Global Health Observatory & Expenditure Database (2022)
- OECD Health Statistics, Education at a Glance, Social Expenditure (2023)
- World Bank World Development Indicators (2022)
- UNODC World Drug Report (2023)

- PISA 2022 Results (OECD)
- Institute for Criminal Policy Research / World Prison Brief
- Country-specific government statistical agencies

### Efficiency Metrics

- **Health:** Life-years per $1,000 spent per capita (USD PPP)
- **Drug Policy:** Composite score (drug deaths 35%, incarceration 25%, treatment access 25%, HIV among PWID 15%)
- **Education:** Average PISA score per % GDP spent on education
- **Criminal Justice:** Composite score (homicide rate 40%, recidivism 35%, incarceration rate 25%)

### Limitations

- Efficiency metrics are simplified; they do not capture equity, quality of life, or distributional effects.
- Cross-country comparisons are confounded by cultural, geographic, demographic, and institutional differences.
- Data availability and measurement methods vary across countries.
- Transferability assessments are qualitative and necessarily approximate.
